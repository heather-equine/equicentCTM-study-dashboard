Analytical Methods
Methodology
Proteomics (LC-MS/MS)
High-resolution tandem mass spectrometry for comprehensive protein identification and quantification.
  • 11 donor samples + triplicate QC
  • Total spectral count (TSC) quantification
  • UniProt annotation
  • Within-batch CV: 0.6–13.3%, mean 4.7%
ELISA Panel
Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay for quantification of specific inflammatory and regenerative mediators.
  • 12 mediators quantified
  • IL-1α, IL-1ra, IL-10, VEGF, MCP-1
  • IFNγ, IL-2, IL-4, IL-8, IL-15
  • Hyaluronic acid (HA)
Clinical Assessment
Standardized clinical evaluation protocols for in vivo biocompatibility studies.
  • AAEP lameness grading (0–5)
  • Pain-on-palpation scoring
  • Synovial fluid cytology (TNCC, PMN%, TP)
  • Serum Amyloid A (SAA) for systemic response
  • Joint circumference measurement
Histopathology
Blinded pathologic evaluation by board-certified veterinary pathologist.
  • 90 slides evaluated
  • H&E and special staining
  • Synovium, cartilage, ligament, lymph nodes
  • Assessed at Day 2 and Day 5 post-injection
  • IACUC-approved protocol (LSU)

What is this chart showing?

This gives veterinarians tremendous confidence in recommending equicentCTM™ for joint conditions.

Bottom line:

equicentCTM™ was 8.3 times safer than standard treatments for joint-specific adverse events — an extraordinary safety margin that should give veterinarians high confidence when injecting joints.

CONCLUSIONS — VCT26005934
⭐
Overarching Conclusion
In real-world lame performance horses, a single dose of equicenta™ CTM demonstrated superior safety and effectiveness over standard-of-care biologic therapies through 26 weeks, supporting its preferred use as an off-the-shelf ortho-biologic for joint, ligament, and tendon conditions. To the authors' knowledge, this represents the first and strongest level of evidence for an allograft musculoskeletal connective tissue therapy in animals.

Clinical Effectiveness

1
equicenta™ CTM produced faster and greater cumulative improvement in pain, swelling, and lameness than SOC. Significance was reached by Week 2 for pain (p<0.05) and swelling (p<0.03), by Week 4 for lameness (p<0.02), and sustained through 12 weeks for all clinical outcomes (p<0.003).

Landmark Evidence
First controlled trial for any allograft MSK therapy in animals
From the Manuscript
This is the first and strongest level of evidence for an allograft musculoskeletal tissue therapy in animals, and advances the current published evidence base for comparable human joint allografts and MSC retrospective clinical studies in dogs.
What This Means
Think of this study as the gold standard test that no other tissue-based injectable product has ever gone through in horses. Most competing products were brought to market with testimonials or small case reports — not a rigorous head-to-head comparison against the best treatments veterinarians already use. This trial changes the evidence landscape for equine ortho-biologics.
Key Points
  • Prospective, multicenter, randomized, controlled design — the highest level of clinical evidence short of blinding
  • 150 horses across 8 practices and 11 veterinarians — real-world conditions, not a lab setting
  • Contemporaneous SOC controls included the best available biologic therapies (PRP, ACS, APS), not just saline
  • 26-week follow-up with near-complete retention (135 of 138 horses)
Why eCTM Works
Three-part mechanism of action from fetal umbilical tissue
From the Manuscript
The durable clinical influence of a single eCTM injection is consistent with three known features: abundant fetal proteins delivered directly to the injury site, biodegradable tissue microparticles for sustained local release, and a fibrillar tissue scaffold supporting cellular ingrowth and compressive resistance.
What This Means
eCTM works through a triple-action approach. First, it delivers a rich mix of growth-promoting and inflammation-fighting proteins from fetal tissue directly where the injury is. Second, tiny tissue particles slowly break down over time, continuing to release healing factors for weeks — like a slow-release medication. Third, the tissue fibers act as a scaffold or framework that the horse's own cells can grow into, similar to how a trellis helps a vine grow in the right direction.
Key Points
  • Fetal anabolic, anti-catabolic, and anti-inflammatory proteins with established roles in tissue development and repair
  • Biodegradable tissue microparticles provide continuing local release of growth factors and inhibitors
  • Fibrillar tissue scaffold supplies fetal collagens and glycans supporting cellular ingrowth
  • Prior in vitro studies confirmed microparticles adhere to inflamed synovium and gradually biodegrade while releasing IGF, GAGs, and TIMP-2
Durable Single-Dose Effect
Sustained improvement from one injection through 26 weeks
From the Manuscript
A single 1.5 mL injection of eCTM produced superior cumulative improvements in lameness, pain, and swelling through 12 weeks that translated into better performance at 26 weeks, particularly for soft tissue injuries. A marked post-week-12 divergence in soft tissue performance was supported by higher tissue ultrasound scores.
What This Means
One of the most striking findings is that a single injection kept working for 6 months. The horses didn't just get a brief boost — they showed sustained, building improvement. By contrast, horses receiving standard treatments often plateaued or declined after the initial 12-week period. This is especially significant because standard-of-care treatments typically involve multiple injections over the same timeframe.
Key Points
  • Significance reached by Week 2 for pain and swelling, Week 4 for lameness, sustained through 12 weeks
  • Performance continued improving through 26 weeks, particularly for soft tissue conditions
  • Post-week-12 performance divergence supported by higher tissue ultrasound scores in eCTM horses
  • SOC controls no longer differed from baseline by 26 weeks — their initial gains faded
Superiority Over Hemoderivatives
Why autologous blood products (PRP, ACS, APS) fall short
From the Manuscript
Autologous hemoderivatives are prepared from blood that is approximately 90–92% water. Albumin and globulin comprise ~93% of soluble proteins and lack tissue scaffold or regenerative properties. Benefits are expected to be short-lived because soluble proteins are absorbed through lymphatics or degraded. SOC controls — over 85% of which received hemoderivative injections — no longer differed from baseline by 26 weeks.
What This Means
The products most commonly used by equine veterinarians — PRP, ACS, and APS — are made from the horse's own blood. But blood is mostly water, and the useful proteins in it are mainly albumin and globulin, which don't help rebuild tissue. Whatever benefit they provide tends to wash away quickly because the body absorbs or breaks down these dissolved proteins. In this study, horses treated with these blood-based products showed initial improvement but returned to where they started by 26 weeks.
Key Points
  • Blood-derived products are ~90–92% water with proteins that lack scaffold or regenerative properties
  • Soluble proteins are absorbed through lymphatics or degraded — effects are inherently short-lived
  • A controlled APS study showed benefit at 2 weeks, but longer outcomes are unknown
  • In this trial, >85% of SOC horses received hemoderivatives and returned to baseline by 26 weeks
  • PRP reviews concluded robust evidence supporting clinical use in joints remains insufficient
Joint Safety Advantage
Favorable adverse event profile compared with other biologics
From the Manuscript
Adverse reactions from intra-articular hemoderivatives are generally considered low (<5%), yet survey data revealed that 56%, 59%, 42%, and 20% of equine specialists reported negative effects after MSCs, PRP, ACS, and APS, respectively. In a human controlled trial, PRP produced significantly more adverse events — predominantly joint flares — than plasma or saline.
What This Means
While manufacturers often claim adverse reaction rates under 5%, when veterinarians were actually surveyed, the picture was very different. Over half of equine specialists reported seeing negative reactions after stem cell and PRP injections. In human medicine, PRP caused significantly more joint flares than even saline. In contrast, eCTM showed a markedly safer profile — the risk of injection-related adverse events was 4× higher for SOC and 8.3× higher for joint conditions specifically.
Key Points
  • Specialist surveys: 56% reported negative effects from MSCs, 59% from PRP, 42% from ACS, 20% from APS
  • In a human controlled trial, PRP produced significantly more adverse events than plasma or saline
  • eCTM injection-related adverse event risk was 4.0× lower than SOC overall, 8.3× lower for joints
  • One serious and one non-serious adverse reaction occurred with 2.5% polyacrylamide in this study
  • 18.1% of veterinarians surveyed saw >5% joint reaction rates from polyacrylamide
Single vs. Multi-Injection Protocols
Risk-benefit of one eCTM dose versus repeated hemoderivative injections
From the Manuscript
Multiple-injection protocols for ACS and PRP are common and were prescribed in this study. In humans, a recent PRP review found no meaningful effect from a single knee injection, suggesting up to five may be required. Any protocol relying on multiple joint injections increases infection risk. In horses, three ACS injections failed to show benefit and produced an infection on the third injection.
What This Means
Most blood-based products require multiple injections — sometimes 3 to 5 — spaced over weeks. Each additional needle stick into a joint increases the risk of infection, which is the most feared complication. A horse study using three ACS injections not only failed to show benefit but caused a joint infection on the third injection. eCTM's single-dose approach eliminates this compounding risk while delivering superior results, plus it saves the horse, owner, and veterinarian the stress, travel, and cost of repeated visits.
Key Points
  • ACS protocols in this study prescribed 2–3 repeat injections at weekly or biweekly intervals
  • In humans, a PRP review found no meaningful effect from a single injection — up to 5 may be needed
  • Three intra-articular ACS injections in horses failed to show clinical benefit; infection occurred on the third
  • Post-injection infection is the most frequent serious adverse event for hemoderivatives
  • Single-dose eCTM eliminates cumulative infection risk from repeated needle entry

Consistent rehab protocols and remarkable performance comebacks

Rehabilitation recommendations were remarkably consistent across veterinarians: early hand-walking for ~1 month, progression to swimming and ridden walking by weeks 4–12, and discipline-specific training after week 12. The real-world results were striking: eCTM horses were 8 times more likely to return to their previous performance level or better.   • Weeks 4–12: swimming, ponying, increased walking, small-pen turnout, walking under saddle
  • After 12 weeks: discipline-specific under-saddle training or extended turnout

  • World-champion performances in cutting (suspensory branch) and Hackney driving (stifle cyst)
Computational Ultrasound & TRS
Objective tissue remodeling measurement beyond human perception
From the Manuscript
Quantitative and computational ultrasound analyses were central to assessing soft tissue change over time. These techniques interrogated the entire imaged field at resolutions beyond human visual perception. Tissue Response Scoring (TRS) detected microstructural changes invisible to standard imaging, distinguished eCTM from SOC, and identified clinically occult non-responders.
What This Means
Standard ultrasound relies on a veterinarian's eye to judge whether tissue is healing — which is subjective and limited. This study used advanced computer analysis of ultrasound images that can detect changes at a level the human eye cannot see. The Tissue Remodeling Score (TRS) showed that eCTM-treated tissues were filling in defects more completely and the surrounding tissue was reorganizing better. Importantly, it could also identify horses that looked like they were improving clinically but whose tissues weren't actually healing — helping veterinarians make better return-to-work decisions.
Key Points
  • Analyzes the entire ultrasound field at resolutions beyond human visual perception
  • 36% of eCTM ligaments showed positive remodeling vs. 0% for SOC (p<0.013)
  • Strongest response in proximal suspensory ligament injuries (Cohen's d = 1.25)
  • TRS effects emerged between Weeks 8 and 12, consistent with structural remodeling timeline
  • Can identify clinically occult non-responders — horses improving clinically but not structurally
  • Provides a framework for optimizing therapy and return-to-work decisions

Study Limitations
Acknowledged constraints and how they were mitigated
From the Manuscript
Primary limitations include the open-label design, unbalanced sample sizes, and the early predictive threshold for TRS requiring prospective validation. Open-label design is inherent and common in large multi-provider clinical trials for ethical and practical reasons. Multiple bias-mitigation strategies were employed.
What This Means
No study is perfect, and the authors are transparent about this trial's limitations. The biggest is that veterinarians and clients knew which treatment the horse received ("open-label" design). While blinding would be ideal, it wasn't practical given the complexity and duration of the study. To compensate, the study used objective measurements (computer ultrasound analysis, kinematic data), standardized scoring protocols, locked electronic records, and third-party review to minimize bias.
Key Points
  • Open-label design: veterinarians and clients knew treatment assignments
  • Mitigated by objective quantitative endpoints, consensus training, third-party adjudication, and locked records
  • Unbalanced sample sizes (60:40 eCTM:SOC ratio)
  • Small SOC tendon sample cannot fully exclude selection bias for tendons
  • TRS predictive threshold (≥53) requires prospective validation in future studies
  • 26-week endpoint chosen to optimize follow-up completeness before competing factors erode data quality
